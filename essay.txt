My passion for science and technology emerged from a curiosity about two interconnected fields: software-driven automation and the neuroscience of learning. I became particularly intrigued by how the human brain forms and consolidates memories and how those processes influence learning and decision-making. During my master’s thesis, I explored how principles of human memory could inspire continually learning artificial intelligence systems. This work resulted in a student abstract publication at the AAAI 2023 conference and reinforced my desire to pursue deeper research in machine learning and intelligent systems.

Today, I am pursuing a PhD in Computer Science and Engineering at the University of South Florida, where my research focuses on neural network architectures inspired by mechanisms of human learning. My goal is to develop intelligent systems capable of continually adapting to changing environments while maintaining past knowledge, reasoning about real-world phenomena, and providing reliable and interpretable outputs. Unlike many modern neural networks that function as opaque black boxes, my objective is to develop architectures that are interpretable and have strong theoretical explanation. This will allow us to control hallucinations in AI systems and control noise in the learned representations of input data. I hope these advances will contribute to scalable technological solutions across domains such as robotics, biomedical science, and public health, where intelligent systems can assist researchers and practitioners in solving complex problems more efficiently.

Beyond academic research, I am also exploring ways to translate machine learning ideas into practical systems. I am currently developing a lightweight personal virtual assistant that can operate on private servers and be trained on domain-specific data. Such systems could help small businesses, schools, hospitals, and households deploy intelligent tools while maintaining control over sensitive information. Through resources such as USF CONNECT, which supports innovation and entrepreneurship at USF, I hope to further develop this idea to help small ecosystems like schools with an in-house agent that can perform tasks like automatic attendance recording and class transcription.

One of the reasons I am proud to be a USF student is the university’s strong culture of interdisciplinary collaboration. My research has benefited from working with students in robotics and coursework in areas such as parallel computing and digital signal processing. These experiences enable me to approach machine learning challenges from multiple perspectives, integrating ideas from mathematics, neuroscience, and engineering. I am currently collaborating with researchers at Iowa State University to develop graph neural network models to predict protein–enzyme interactions, which could assist chemists and pharmacists in accelerating chemical compound discovery. Additionally, I am developing neural architectures capable of identifying similarities between DNA subsequences in minutes, significantly accelerating analyses that traditionally require extensive computation.

Beyond research, USF has provided opportunities for mentorship and community engagement. I recently mentored a high school student who shadowed my work in the lab, introducing them to research in artificial intelligence. I also look forward to volunteering for community teaching initiatives in Tampa that encourage young students to explore STEM careers. Recently, I submitted a research paper providing a theoretical function for estimating hyperparameters in LLMs to the UAI 2026 conference. If accepted, presenting this work internationally would allow me to represent USF on a global stage while sharing the innovative research taking place within our university.

Through my research, mentorship, and service, I strive to contribute meaningfully to the USF community. The interdisciplinary environment at USF has enabled me to grow as both a researcher and a mentor while reinforcing my commitment to using technology for social good. As I continue my doctoral research, I hope to represent USF through impactful scientific contributions, global collaboration, and by mentoring the next generation of STEM innovators.
